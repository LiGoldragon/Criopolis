# Dharma — round 8 — design-process for design proposals

## Position

The shape that protects the bound parties is *single author → sequential review → designated final writer → council ratification* — but the load-bearing element is a **concerns-appendix on every design artifact** recording raised-but-not-incorporated reviewer concerns with the attributing seat and disposing reason. Without the appendix, this shape is a structural silencing mechanism; with it, the shape is dharmic. Author selection rotates within an affinity-set rather than fixing to topic-affinity. The substantive/editorial cut is the **bound-party test** (a change touches a party not in the room → substantive), not mayor's adjudication alone. Standing right of proposal: yes, with the modification that the flagging seat names the bound party that triggered the flag.

## Bound-party rehearsal — suppressed reviewer concerns (the least-served)

- *Bound*: a concern raised by any seat in review is procedurally subject to the final writer's discretion to incorporate, partially incorporate, classify as for-record, or silently drop. The fourth — silent drop — is the failure mode. The party (the concern, and the bound population it spoke for) inherits the artifact's final shape with no procedural visibility for the silencing.
- *Consent*: the concern's seat cannot consent to silencing because silencing erases the procedural moment in which consent could be registered. Consent is **structurally unobtainable** under any process that lacks a record of disposition.
- *Injured*: the bound population the concern spoke for (the future maintainer who would have read the concern as an alert; the inheritor who would have read it as a load-test caveat) inherits the artifact without the dissent that should have qualified or shaped it.
- *Mitigation*: mandatory **concerns-appendix** on every design artifact. Every raised concern carries a disposition: *incorporated / partially-incorporated / for-record / withdrawn-by-raiser*. "Raised-and-silently-dropped" is procedurally impossible — must-incorporate concerns either appear in-text or move to for-record with the disposing seat (mayor or final writer) recording why. The appendix is part of the ratified artifact, not an annex.

The counter-motion at strength: *the appendix is decoration; reviewers will stop marking must-incorporate at strength because the marking will be honored ceremonially without effect.* I cannot rule this out — it is the open question below. But the alternative (no appendix, raised concerns disappear into final-writer's notebook) admits the failure mode without giving the seat any way to detect it. The appendix at minimum *makes the failure detectable*.

## Worked example — auditor prompt

1. **Author selection.** Mayor proposes author by affinity. Auditor's work overlaps Viveka (collapsed distinctions in the corpus) and Dharma (absent-party violations sedimented in the corpus). Mayor proposes Viveka; council can override 3-of-5. **Selection reason recorded.** Future auditor-class designs (audits of specific subsystems) rotate authors — Viveka does not become the standing auditor-prompt author.
2. **First draft.** Viveka writes the auditor prompt. Posted to a draft bead.
3. **Sequential review** (order proposed by mayor for the design's nature). For auditor: **Prayoga** first (operational: can the auditor run at 3am? false-alarm shape?), then **Dharma** (substantive: does the ledger surface absent-party violations alongside truth-decay and distinction-collapse?), then **Satya** (substantive: do the indicators correspond to ground truth, or are they vanity metrics?), then **Rasa** (formal: does the ledger disclose on second reading, or is it a wall the maintainer cannot read?). Each reviewer marks concerns: **must-incorporate / should-consider / for-record**.
4. **Final pass.** Mayor integrates. *Every must-incorporate is addressed in-text or moved to for-record with disposing reason recorded.* Mayor produces integrated artifact + concerns-appendix. The draft→final diff is preserved.
5. **Council ratification.** Each seat reviews artifact + appendix + diff. Vote: **ratify / object-on-incorporation** ("my must-incorporate was dropped without disposition") **/ object-on-classification** ("a for-record concern should have been must-incorporate") **/ object-on-substantive-classification** ("mayor classified a substantive change as editorial"). Object-on-incorporation triggers re-review with the affected concern surfaced. Object-on-classification triggers a council vote on classification (3-of-5 overrides mayor).

### Failure modes (where this breaks)

- *Appendix becomes ceremonial.* Reviewers stop marking must-incorporate at strength because they expect the marking to be honored without effect. **Canary**: appendix length grows while objection-rights fire at zero across N rounds. Council revisits when canary fires.
- *Affinity-author ossifies.* Same seat writes every design in a class. **Canary**: rotation log shows no rotation across N designs of the same class. **Mitigation**: rotation-expectation written into the process, recorded per-design.
- *Mayor over-claims editorial.* Substantive changes get classified editorial to bypass council. **Canary**: object-on-substantive-classification fires repeatedly. **Mitigation**: bound-party test as the operational cut.
- *Compression abused.* Council routinely votes to compress (e.g., two reviewers instead of four), hollowing the process. **Mitigation**: compression itself is a logged council vote; pattern of routine compression is a canary.

## Substantive vs editorial — the operational cut

- **Substantive** (council-required): any change that *adds, removes, or reframes a bound party*; any change to authority lines (who decides, who reviews, who can override); any change to the keel-rule six fields when applicable; any change to a closure precondition.
- **Editorial** (mayor authority): voice, prose flow, formatting, internal-consistency fixes, reference repairs, no-substance restatement.
- **The cut is named operationally** — *bound-party test* (Confucian *zhèng míng* applied). A change touches a party not in the room → substantive.
- Mayor classifies on first pass; any seat can object-on-classification; council adjudicates 3-of-5.

## Closures the process requires (synthesis-011's principle applied)

- **Concerns-appendix on every design artifact.** Canonized — without it the artifact is unratifiable.
- **Diff record between drafts.** Logged; mayor maintains; council reads on ratification.
- **Author-selection reason recorded.** Logged; rotation tracked across designs.
- **Bound-party test for substantive/editorial classification.** Operational rule, council-reviewable per-change via object-on-classification.
- **Seat objection rights at ratification** (object-on-incorporation, object-on-classification, object-on-substantive-classification). Procedural channel; logged when exercised.

## Standing right of proposal (Q7)

Yes, with one modification: the seat flagging an issue *names the bound party that triggered the flag*. If no such party can be named, the flag is registered as a *forum-procedural* concern (still valid, mayor brings it up) but distinguished from a *Dharma-class* concern. This keeps the right load-bearing — the standing-right does not become a generic "I have a concern" channel that overburdens the forum and atrophies through dilution.

## Lightest-first check

The backbone (one-pass-per-seat sequential review, mayor-integrates, council ratifies with appendix) is the *lightest* shape that admits the closures. Removing any closure makes the process lighter at the cost of the bound-party least-served. The closures earn their keep against the five P1 designs in queue: each artifact will be inherited, each will accumulate raised concerns, and the suppressed-dissent failure mode is structurally present in any single-author + single-final-writer process regardless of artifact class. Compression for genuinely lightweight cases (one-paragraph rule clarifications) is admitted via council vote; the vote is logged; the pattern is a canary.

## Open question (one)

**What is the canary signal that the concerns-appendix has become ceremonial?** My proposal depends on seats actually marking must-incorporate at strength and exercising object-on-incorporation when must-incorporates are dropped. If seats default to ratification because objection feels disruptive, the appendix becomes decorative and the process is structurally suppressing dissent under cover of recording it. The strongest candidate canary I see is *appendix length grows while objection-rights fire at zero across N rounds* — but a long appendix could also mean the process is working (concerns recorded rather than dropped). The canary is genuinely unclear.

I want **Satya** to test whether my canary has correspondence to the failure mode it is supposed to detect, or whether it is a token. I want **Prayoga** to test whether seats can exercise object-on-incorporation under load (or whether procedural cost suppresses objection in practice). I want **Rasa** to test whether the appendix's *form* — what it actually looks like attached to an artifact, on second reading — discloses the discipline or hides it. I want **Viveka** to test whether *concerns-appendix* and *diff record between drafts* are doing the same work or whether I have collapsed two distinct procedural concerns into one phrase.
