A doctor is deciding whether to order one more scan before sending a patient home. More information may help; more delay may harm. The good of the decision will not appear as a slogan about prudence. It will appear in whether the patient is neither abandoned to risk nor held captive by anxiety.

**Position**

Lead with balance. Balance is the lived fitting of response to situation, where several goods can injure one another when isolated. Truth, beauty, elegance, and wholeness are not synonyms for balance, but balance is where they become answerable to life: truth tells the response what it must not deny; beauty lets fittingness be perceived; elegance removes needless burden; wholeness asks whether the fit still holds when the surrounding relations and time are admitted. The shared primordial word is fit, but fit is not convenience. It is the accountable relation between what is, what is needed, what can be borne, and what can continue.

**Operational check**

The decision this changes is where inquiry stops and action begins. The distinction that must survive contact is balance versus compromise: compromise splits claims to reduce conflict; balance finds the response in which each claim keeps its proper weight. The cost is judgment: one must attend to circumstance, proportion, timing, and consequence instead of hiding inside a rule. The failure mode made less likely is overcorrection: the true but cruel answer, the beautiful but false gesture, the elegant but brittle reduction, the whole that erases the wounded part. Remaining uncertainty is local: several non-library citations need librarian verification, but the working structure is strong enough to ship as a cornerstone draft.

**Five essences**

**Balance.** Balance is the active fitting of more and less, firmness and yielding, so that the goods of a situation remain mutually serviceable rather than devouring one another.

Source anchors: Aristotle's virtue as a mean relative to us, between excess and defect, determined by practical reason (*Nicomachean Ethics* II.6, 1106b36-1107a8, Ross/Oxford; paraphrase from memory; flag for librarian). Patanjali's *sthira-sukham asanam* makes posture neither rigid nor slack but steady-easeful (*Yoga Sutra* II.46; paraphrase from memory; flag for librarian). Plotinus gives the same structure cosmically: a function may be bitter in itself and still needed for the animal whole; some balance of function is indispensable to unity and total harmony (*Enneads* II.3.5, MacKenna, `library/en/plotinus/six-enneads-mackenna-page-2.html` A704-A714), while unbalanced vital energy falls into ferocity or faint-heartedness (*Enneads* II.3.11, A871-A878).

Distinguishing question: When two real goods pull apart, what response lets each keep its proper claim without pretending their claims are equal?

**Truth.** Truth is the correction by which a belief, word, or perception is brought under the pressure of what it is about, until it can be relied on without self-deception.

Source anchors: James treats truth pragmatically as what becomes verified in the course of experience, not as a dead label attached outside life (*Pragmatism*, lecture VI, "Pragmatism's Conception of Truth"; paraphrase from memory; flag for librarian). Dewey's "warranted assertibility" makes truth the settled outcome of disciplined inquiry, not private certainty (*Logic: The Theory of Inquiry*, chs. 6-7; paraphrase from memory; flag for librarian). Plotinus' dialectic recognizes falsity as a clash with its canon of truth and knows truth beyond mere strings of assertions (*Enneads* I.3.4, MacKenna, `library/en/plotinus/six-enneads-mackenna-page-1.html` A839-A860).

Distinguishing question: What would force me to revise this statement if I loved my present version?

**Beauty.** Beauty is the felt disclosure of fit: a form, act, or relation appears worthy because its parts, measure, and life answer one another.

Source anchors: Plotinus' musician is drawn by tones, rhythm, design, measure, and shapely pattern, then must be led from particular beauties toward the principle making them intelligible (*Enneads* I.3.1, MacKenna, `library/en/plotinus/six-enneads-mackenna-page-1.html` A757-A787). Dewey treats art as experience brought to consummation, where doing, undergoing, rhythm, and fulfillment belong together (*Art as Experience*, ch. 3, "Having an Experience"; paraphrase from memory; flag for librarian).

Distinguishing question: Does the thing merely please, or does sustained attention disclose more relation, proportion, and life?

**Elegance.** Elegance is sufficiency without residue: the form answers what the situation asks while carrying no excess that must be defended for its own sake.

Source anchors: Plotinus' account of virtue as bound and measure gives elegance its moral analogue: the measured is lifted out of the unmeasured and lawless (*Enneads* I.2.2, MacKenna, `library/en/plotinus/six-enneads-mackenna-page-1.html` A504-A516). Poincare links mathematical beauty to hidden harmony and economy of means; Hardy similarly treats serious beauty as involving inevitability and surprise without waste (*Science and Method*, Book I; Hardy, *A Mathematician's Apology*, sections 10-18; paraphrase from memory; flag for librarian).

Distinguishing question: If one part is removed, is something essential lost; if one part is added, does the form become worse?

**Wholeness.** Wholeness is the integrity by which parts become mutually intelligible across relation and time, including tensions that a smaller view would call disorder.

Source anchors: Plotinus insists that parts serve the whole as organs serve a living body; only through such relation can there be unity and total harmony (*Enneads* II.3.5, MacKenna, `library/en/plotinus/six-enneads-mackenna-page-2.html` A704-A714). In *On Providence*, he says every greater includes lesser things and that a whole has parts; even conflict must be considered in mutual relationship and brought into an ordered total where possible (*Enneads* III.2.14-15, MacKenna, `library/en/plotinus/six-enneads-mackenna-page-3.html` A855-A905). Buber helps name the interpersonal form: relation is not added after the self is complete; the I is formed differently in genuine address (*I and Thou*, Part I; paraphrase from memory; flag for librarian).

Distinguishing question: Does this account preserve the part's own dignity while showing why it belongs with the others?

**Relations among the five**

They are five tests of fit, not five ornamental names for one vague good. Truth is fit to what is. Beauty is fit as perceived and loved. Elegance is fit under restraint, where no extra burden remains. Wholeness is fit across the full field of relation and time. Balance is fit in the act of choosing, adjusting, refusing, waiting, or proceeding.

Balance leads for this seat because the other four can become vices when unbalanced. Truth without balance can become cruelty or paralysis: it keeps adding qualifications after action is due, or states a fact with no regard for the person who must receive it. Beauty without truth becomes seduction. Elegance without wholeness becomes amputation. Wholeness without the local correction of truth becomes a grand story that excuses damage. Balance is not superior as a doctrine; it is prior as a lived test.

The topology is therefore not a ladder but a cycle. Truth corrects balance. Balance protects beauty from sentimentality. Beauty protects elegance from mere thinness. Elegance protects wholeness from swollen inclusion. Wholeness returns balance to the wider field, where today's fitting act must still answer tomorrow.

The five do not collapse into "what survives daily contact," but daily contact is the test that exposes impostors. A falsehood may survive by force, a beautiful form may survive by fashion, an elegant form may survive because no one has asked whom it excludes, and a whole may survive as a name while its parts are damaged. The better formula is: each essence must change conduct under contact with reality, relation, and time.

**Cost ledger / decision**

This cornerstone costs a refusal of easy absolutes. It asks later readers to judge proportion instead of citing a single sovereign value. That is expensive in attention, but cheap compared with the damage caused by treating one good as if it cancels the rest. Ship this as the Pragmatist base: balance first, fit as the common grammar, the five as distinct tests.

The stop rule is local. The non-library citations need librarian verification, especially Aristotle, Patanjali, James, Dewey, Buber, Poincare, and Hardy. That verification can change wording and anchors; it should not change the working relation unless a source directly contradicts the fitted structure.

**Worked example**

A teacher is explaining grief to a child whose friend has moved away. Truth says not to promise that nothing is lost. Beauty says the words should have gentleness and rhythm enough to be received. Elegance says not to give a lecture when one clear sentence and a held silence will do. Wholeness says the child's sadness, the absent friend, the future possibility of new friendship, and the teacher's authority all belong in the moment. Balance is the teacher's act: enough truth to honor the loss, enough softness to keep the child from being crushed, enough brevity to leave room for feeling, enough presence to let the moment continue.

**Open question**

Is balance itself primordial, or is it the human mode of encountering a deeper order whose names are truth, beauty, elegance, and wholeness? My answer here treats balance as prior in lived practice, not necessarily prior in being.
